# Notes on Black Holes

- Black holes typically form when massive stars collapse at the end of their life cycle.
- After formation, black holes can grow by absorbing mass from their surroundings.
- The boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing can escape is called the event horizon.
- Black holes have an event horizon, a boundary beyond which nothing can escape, not even light.
- The center of a black hole is called the singularity, where density and gravity become infinite.
- Black holes can be detected by their effect on nearby stars and gas, emitting X-rays.
- There are different types of black holes: stellar, intermediate, and supermassive.
- Supermassive black holes exist at the centers of most galaxies, including our Milky Way.
- The study of black holes helps in understanding fundamental physics, including gravity and quantum mechanics.
- Black holes can merge, producing gravitational waves detectable by instruments like LIGO.
- Recent research continues to explore black hole properties, their formation, and their impact on the universe.

# Notes on Black Holes

- Black holes are extremely dense pockets of matter with incredible mass concentrated in a very small volume.
- They drastically warp the fabric of space-time around them due to their gravitational pull.
- Some black holes lie dormant but can wake briefly to feed on passing stars.
- Gravitational waves from black holes may provide clues on the location of dark matter.
- Recent studies have found more active black holes in dwarf galaxies than previously thought.
- Black holes can be classified based on their size, such as dwarf black holes and those the size of the Milky Way.
- Researchers are actively studying black holes to understand their role in galaxy formation and evolution.
- Observations of black holes contribute to our knowledge of fundamental physics and the nature of gravity.
- Black hole mergers are detected through gravitational wave signals, revealing information about their properties.
- The study of black holes involves multiple astronomical techniques including X-ray observations and theoretical modeling.
